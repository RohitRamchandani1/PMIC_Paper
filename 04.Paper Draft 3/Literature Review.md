# Literature Review: PMIC-Based Software-Controlled Power Management for STM32MP1 Embedded Systems

## Scope

This literature review focuses on verified sources relevant to software-controlled power management for STM32MP1-class embedded systems using STPMIC1/STPMIC1L PMICs. It prioritizes peer-reviewed IEEE/ACM work on dynamic voltage scaling (DVS), dynamic voltage and frequency scaling (DVFS), and dynamic power management (DPM), together with official STMicroelectronics datasheets, reference manuals, board documentation, and application notes. The sources below are limited to papers and documents with a DOI or an official publisher/vendor URL.

## Synthesis of the Literature

Embedded SoCs reduce power through coordinated control of voltage domains, clocks, and operating states. The theoretical foundation for voltage scaling is that CMOS dynamic power is approximately proportional to $C V^2 f$, so reducing supply voltage can reduce dynamic energy when the workload and timing constraints permit it. Yao, Demers, and Shenker formalized the scheduling problem for reducing CPU energy by varying processor speed, showing that energy-aware scheduling depends on workload timing constraints rather than only on hardware capability [1]. Later DVFS work moved this idea into operating systems and embedded processors. Pillai and Shin demonstrated real-time dynamic voltage scaling in an embedded operating-system context [2], while Pering, Burd, and Brodersen evaluated DVS algorithms and highlighted the importance of policy choice and workload behavior [3]. Burd et al. then showed a complete dynamic-voltage-scaled microprocessor system, making voltage-scaled hardware a practical implementation target rather than only a scheduling abstraction [4].

Dynamic power management broadens the problem from CPU voltage alone to coordinated decisions about device states, idle intervals, and wake-up costs. Benini, Bogliolo, and De Micheli surveyed system-level DPM techniques and emphasized that energy reduction requires policy mechanisms able to transition components between active and low-power states [5]. Simunic et al. studied portable-system DPM and later combined voltage scaling with power management for portable systems, showing that software policy and hardware state-transition support must be considered together [6], [7]. These papers support the central claim that software-controlled low-power modes are meaningful only when the hardware exposes controllable states and when the software accounts for latency, energy cost, and workload timing.

For embedded and real-time systems, DVFS cannot be treated as an isolated voltage-control feature. Pouwelse, Langendoen, and Sips evaluated DVS on a low-power microprocessor and connected voltage scaling to practical software control on constrained platforms [8]. Zhong and Xu addressed energy-aware modeling and scheduling for real-time tasks under DVS [9]. Bhatti, Belleudy, and Auguin later examined hybrid power management in real-time embedded systems, explicitly treating DVFS and DPM as interacting techniques rather than independent optimizations [10]. These works are directly relevant to STM32MP1 power-mode experiments because they caution against interpreting voltage reduction alone as proof of system-level energy savings. Voltage traces support claims about rail behavior and DVS function, but current, latency, workload, and duty-cycle measurements are needed to claim total energy savings.

The STM32MP1 platform adds a multi-rail power-delivery problem. STMicroelectronics documentation describes STM32MP1 devices as Arm Cortex-A based MPUs with multiple functional domains and low-power operating states controlled through the device power architecture [11], [12]. On an STM32MP135F-DK board, ST identifies the STPMIC1 as the onboard PMIC and lists board-level features such as the STM32MP135FA MPU, DDR3L memory, USB, Ethernet, wireless connectivity, and onboard current measurement [13]. This board-level evidence supports using the STM32MP135F-DK as a practical target for PMIC-controlled experiments, but it does not by itself quantify energy reduction.

STPMIC1 and STPMIC1L documentation provides the PMIC-specific basis for the paper. The STPMIC1 datasheet and product page describe a highly integrated PMIC for microprocessor units, including four buck converters, LDOs, a boost converter, power switches, programmable non-volatile memory, and I2C/digital I/O control [14]. ST states that the STPMIC1 is designed for products based on highly integrated application processors requiring low power and high efficiency, and that its advanced low-power features are controlled by a host processor through I2C and I/O interfaces [14]. STPMIC1 application notes listed by ST include the I2C programming guide, STM32MP151/153/157 battery-powered integration guidance, STM32MP13x/STPMIC1 wall-adapter integration guidance, auto turn-on, and PCB layout guidelines [15]-[19]. These documents support claims about the intended integration path and software-visible PMIC control, but they should be cited as vendor documentation rather than peer-reviewed evidence.

The STPMIC1L is a newer, lower-output-count PMIC positioned by ST for STM32MP1x applications requiring low power and high efficiency. ST describes the STPMIC1L as integrating two buck converters, four LDOs, programmable NVM, I2C and digital I/O control, programmable output voltages, programmable turn-on/off sequences, and immediate alternate output settings through dedicated power-control pins [20]. STPMIC1L application notes listed by ST cover STM32MP13 wall-adapter use, NVM configuration, application hints, bill-of-material details, and PCB layout guidelines [21]-[25]. These sources support claims about STPMIC1L rail programmability, sequencing support, and integration guidance for STM32MP13-class boards.

Taken together, the literature supports a careful framing of an STM32MP1/STPMIC1 experiment. Peer-reviewed DVFS and DPM papers justify why software-controlled voltage and state transitions matter. ST datasheets, reference manuals, board documentation, and application notes justify that the STM32MP1/STPMIC1 hardware exposes the required PMIC, rail, and low-power control mechanisms. However, the reviewed sources do not support unsupported claims such as guaranteed system-level energy savings from voltage measurements alone. A rigorous paper should distinguish between measured rail-voltage behavior, PMIC command behavior, power-mode entry/exit behavior, and whole-board energy outcomes.

## Implications for the STM32MP1/STPMIC1 Paper

The strongest supportable technical claim is that the STM32MP1/STPMIC1 platform provides a software-accessible PMIC path for rail configuration, sequencing, and low-power-mode transitions, and that oscilloscope captures can validate whether selected rails change as commanded. The reviewed literature also supports the relevance of DVS/DVFS and DPM as established low-power techniques.

The current paper should avoid claiming system-level energy savings unless current or power is measured. A voltage reduction on one rail supports a DVS claim for that rail, but total energy depends on load current, clock frequency, regulator efficiency, transition latency, wake-up cost, workload, and time spent in each mode. For conference readiness, the literature points to four additional evidence categories: whole-board power, transition/wake latency, repeatability statistics, and workload-level energy/performance measurements.
